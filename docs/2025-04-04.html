I just wanted to say first—your self-awareness and vulnerability in writing this are really powerful. You’re putting words to something that’s incredibly hard to describe, especially when the emotions feel so much bigger than language can hold. That in itself is a huge step forward.

Here are a few gentle thoughts and suggestions that might help:

1. Create a “translation” layer for your emotions

It sounds like the moment your emotions get activated, it becomes overwhelming fast—and that’s so relatable for those of us who deal with abandonment sensitivity. One way to approach this might be to create a script or even a “note to self/others” that translates your inner experience before the emotional flood hits. Something like:

“When I ask for reassurance or connection, it’s not because I doubt you or don’t trust our relationship. It’s because my brain interprets distance or silence as danger, and I’m trying to rewire that pattern.”

You can write this out ahead of time and even let the other person read it when you're not in crisis. That gives them context for later—and gives you a way to speak for yourself when it's hardest to speak at all.

2. Use sensation anchors to regulate in the moment

When the wave is rising, grounding through physical anchors (cold water, describing what you see/hear/touch, feeling your feet on the floor) can help slow the emotional flood just enough to stay in the driver’s seat.

3. Ask for “pre-consent” to emotional transparency

Sometimes it helps to ask your partner something like: “If I notice I’m spiraling, can I ask for a five-minute check-in where we just pause and reset? I don’t need solutions—just space to share what’s coming up.” This helps you both feel like you're in it together rather than bracing for impact.

4. On “normal” people

The truth is, even “normal” people (whatever that means) struggle with vulnerability. They’re just often socialized to suppress it. What you’re trying to do—name the scary feelings, show up with truth, risk being misunderstood—is incredibly brave. It’s not about being “normal.” It’s about being real. And that’s way harder and more courageous than faking composure.

Lastly:

You’re not broken. You’re feeling deeply in a world that often demands numbness. That comes with pain—but also with strength. You’re doing the work. Be gentle with yourself.
